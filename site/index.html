Four critical levers
How fast we build — balanced
Pace

The case for speed: Epoch AI's capability index has climbed ~15.5 points a year since 2024, with training compute growing ~5× annually — opportunity that compounds. The case for deliberation: reported AI incidents keep setting records, and Stuart Russell calls the race a prisoner's dilemma — everyone would be better off slowing until safety is solved, yet each actor feels compelled to keep racing.

Sources: Epoch AI · Trends · AI Incident Database · Russell (2026)
DeliberateFull speed
Who gets the keys — balanced
Access

Stanford's 2026 AI Index finds both poles in motion. Five hyperscalers now control over two-thirds of global AI compute, and model transparency is falling — yet generative AI reached ~53% of people within three years, faster than the PC or the internet. Few hands promise control and accountability; many hands spread the gains — and the risks.

Sources: Stanford HAI · 2026 AI Index · 12 takeaways

How tightly we govern — balanced
Governance

The OECD AI Principles — the first intergovernmental AI standard, now adhered to by 47 countries plus the EU — name the tension directly: AI should be both innovative and trustworthy. Hands off bets that rules can't keep pace with the technology and would slow discovery; strong oversight bets that transparency, accountability, and safety standards are what make AI durable enough to trust.

Sources: OECD AI Principles · OECD.AI Policy Observatory
Hands offStrong oversight
What we build it for — balanced
Priority

Profit funded today's frontier — but in Human Compatible, Stuart Russell warns that optimizing any narrow, fixed objective invites the King Midas problem: we get what we asked for, not what we meant. Brian Christian's The Alignment Problem traces how systems learn what we specify, not what we intend — and AI Now's 2025 landscape report argues the market is already tilted "heads I win, tails you lose."

Sources: Human Compatible · The Alignment Problem · AI Now (2025)

Potential preset paths

This is a thinking tool, not a forecast. The directions each lever pushes reflect common themes in expert discussion about AI's effects. The math underneath is deliberately simple and transparent, so the tradeoffs stay visible rather than hidden behind false precision. The real point: notice that no lever is purely good, and that how we steer matters as much as how powerful the technology becomes.
